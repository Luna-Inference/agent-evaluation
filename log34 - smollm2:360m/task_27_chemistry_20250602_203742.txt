SN2 Nucleophilic Substitution Mechanism:                                                                                                                                         
                                                                                                                                                                                 
The SN2 (Sn1) nomenclature is used for organic reactions involving an alpha-hydrogen in a base, which abstracts the hydrogen atom, forming a leaving group. This reaction        
involves the following steps:                                                                                                                                                    
                                                                                                                                                                                 
1. The nucleophile (base) attacks the carbonyl carbon of the electrophile (carbon and oxygen), forming a new bond between them.                                                  
2. The electrophile is then protonated to form an alpha-hydrogen ion, which can act as a base in subsequent reactions.                                                           
3. The nucleophile abstracts the hydrogen atom from the alpha-hydrogen, forming a leaving group (-OH) on the carbonyl carbon.                                                    
4. The newly formed leaving group is removed by an acid or water, resulting in a product that has two possible stereocenters: R and S.                                           
                                                                                                                                                                                 
Predicting Stereochemistry of the Product using Mechanism:                                                                                                                       
                                                                                                                                                                                 
1. The SN2 mechanism involves the formation of a leaving group (-OH) on the carbonyl carbon. Since this leaves a hydrogen atom (H), it can be a hydrogen atom or a hydrogen ion  
(H). As there are two possible positions for the hydrogen in these species, we have two stereocenters: R and S.                                                                  
2. If we consider the base as an alpha-hydrogen that abstracts a hydrogen from the carbonyl carbon of the electrophile, then it is likely to be a hydrogen atom. In this case,   
the product has only one stereocenter, so there will be no additional stereo asymmetry in the product.                                                                           
3. However, if we consider the base as a protonated alpha-hydrogen, which abstracts the hydrogen from the alpha-carbon of the electrophile, then it is possible to have multiple 
stereocenters. In this case, the two resulting products would have the same stereochemistry (R or S), indicating that the base can act in both R and S configurations.           
4. Therefore, the SN2 mechanism predicts that the product will have an equal number of stereocenters: two R and two S. This is because the nucleophile attacks from either side  
of the alpha-hydrogen, forming a leaving group (-OH) on each carbonyl carbon, resulting in two stereocenters.                                                                    
                                                                                                                                                                                 
In summary, SN2 nucleophilic substitution produces products with equal numbers of stereocenters: one R or one S. The base can act in both R and S configurations, giving rise to 
two different products with the same stereochemistry (equal number of R and S).
