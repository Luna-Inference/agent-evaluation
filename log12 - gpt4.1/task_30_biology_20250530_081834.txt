CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing technology harnesses a natural defense system from bacteria. The molecular mechanism involves two key components: the Cas9 nuclease protein and a guide RNA (gRNA). Here's how it works:

1. The guide RNA is engineered to be complementary to a specific target DNA sequence in the genome.
2. Cas9 binds the guide RNA and together they scan the cell's DNA for a matching sequence adjacent to a protospacer adjacent motif (PAM).
3. When the target sequence is found, the guide RNA binds to it by Watson-Crick base pairing, and Cas9 induces a double-strand break (DSB) in the DNA.
4. The cell recognizes the break and repairs it via either non-homologous end joining (NHEJ), which often disrupts the gene, or by homology-directed repair (HDR) if a template is provided, allowing precise sequence changes.

This precise targeting and cleavage mechanism enables efficient and versatile gene editing in living cells.
