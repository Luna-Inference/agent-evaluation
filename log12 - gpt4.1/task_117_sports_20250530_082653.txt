**The Beginnings of Baseball Statistics**
-----------------------------------------

The story of baseball analytics begins in the 19th century when statistics were first used to record and evaluate player performance. Alexander Cartwright, often credited as one
of the founding fathers of baseball, was among the first to see the value in documenting game statistics. By the 1870s, box scores were being published in newspapers, providing 
fans with a basic breakdown of a game’s events.

The earliest forms of baseball statistics were fairly rudimentary. Batting average (BA), for instance, was one straightforward metric used to evaluate a player’s hitting 
performance. Developed in the 19th century, this statistic simply calculates the ratio of a player’s hits to their at-bats. ERA (Earned Run Average) was another early metric, 
giving fans and analysts a way to evaluate pitchers based on the number of earned runs they allowed per nine innings.

However, these basic metrics had their limitations. While useful, they often failed to capture the nuances and complexities of the game. For instance, a high batting average did
not always translate to a high run production. Similarly, ERA did not always accurately reflect a pitcher’s skill, especially if they played in a ballpark that was particularly 
favorable or unfavorable to pitchers.

Key Concepts of Sabermetrics
----------------------------

The core of sabermetrics lies in its innovative metrics and formulas. One of the most crucial is On-Base Plus Slugging (OPS), which combines a player’s ability to reach base 
with their power-hitting capacity. Another key measurement is Wins Above Replacement (WAR), an all-encompassing stat that estimates a player’s total contributions to their team,
compared to a replacement-level player.

Fielding Independent Pitching (FIP) is another significant metric, isolating a pitcher’s performance from his defense, emphasizing outcomes under the pitcher’s control such as 
home runs, strikeouts, and walks. Other popular sabermetric metrics include Weighted Runs Created Plus (wRC+) and Batting Average on Balls in Play (BABIP).

Each of these metrics offers a more nuanced understanding of player performance, highlighting areas where past analytics might misrepresent a player’s value. By utilizing these 
comprehensive tools, teams can make more informed decisions about lineup configurations, player contracts, and in-game strategies.
