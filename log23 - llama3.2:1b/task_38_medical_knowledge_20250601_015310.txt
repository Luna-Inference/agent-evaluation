**Genetic Factors:**

1. **Insulin Resistance:** T2DM is characterized by insulin resistance, where the body's cells become less responsive to insulin, a hormone produced by the pancreas that regulates blood sugar levels.
2. **Pancreatic Beta-Cell Failure:** Over time, pancreatic beta-cells, which produce insulin, can be damaged or destroyed, leading to decreased insulin production and further insulin resistance.

**Environmental Factors:**

1. **Obesity:** Excess body fat, particularly around the abdominal area, is a significant risk factor for developing T2DM.
2. **Physical Inactivity:** Lack of regular physical activity contributes to insulin resistance and increases the risk of developing T2DM.
3. **Diet:** A diet high in refined carbohydrates, sugar, and saturated fats can contribute to insulin resistance and increase the risk of developing T2DM.
4. **Age:** The risk of developing T2DM increases with age, particularly after the age of 45.

**Other Factors:**

1. **Genetic Predisposition:** Certain genetic variants, such as those affecting the INFA1, PTPN11, and SLC30A8 genes, can increase the risk of developing T2DM.
2. **Hormonal Imbalance:** Hormonal imbalances, particularly insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1) and cortisol, can contribute to insulin resistance and T2DM.

**Pathophysiological Mechanisms:**

1. **Increased Inflammation:** Chronic inflammation in the body's tissues, including the pancreas, can lead to insulin resistance.
2. **Gut Microbiome Imbalance:** An imbalance of the gut microbiome, which includes changes in the composition and function of gut bacteria, can contribute to insulin resistance and T2DM.
3. **Oxidative Stress:** Chronic oxidative stress, resulting from an imbalance between free radical production and antioxidant defenses, can damage pancreatic beta-cells and contribute to insulin resistance.
