The 2008 financial crisis was the result of a convergence of several interrelated factors. Aggressive subprime mortgage lending led banks to extend credit to high-risk borrowers, fueling a housing bubble that was underpinned by overly optimistic valuations fueled by easy credit. At the same time, financial innovations such as mortgage-backed securities (MBS) and collateralized debt obligations (CDO) obscured the true risk, as these complex instruments dispersed the risk among financial institutions globally.
