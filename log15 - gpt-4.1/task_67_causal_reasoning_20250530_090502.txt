The 2008 Financial Crisis: Causes, Impact, and Lessons Learned
==============================================================

Explore the causes, impacts, and lessons from the 2008 financial crisis, and understand its long-term economic consequences.

[![](https://accountinginsights.org/wp-content/uploads/fly-images/194842/1704746803-2-64x64.png)](/users/2/profile/accountinginsights-team)

[AccountingInsights Team](/users/2/profile/accountinginsights-team)

Published Sep 26, 2024

The 2008 financial crisis stands as one of the most significant economic downturns since the Great Depression. It reshaped global economies, altered financial regulations, and 
left a lasting impact on millions of lives.

Understanding this crisis is crucial for grasping how interconnected our financial systems are and recognizing the vulnerabilities that can lead to widespread economic turmoil.

Causes of the 2008 Financial Crisis
-----------------------------------

The roots of the 2008 financial crisis can be traced back to a combination of factors that created a perfect storm in the global economy. At the heart of the crisis was the 
housing bubble, fueled by an era of easy credit and lax lending standards. Financial institutions, driven by the pursuit of higher profits, began issuing subprime mortgages to 
borrowers with poor credit histories. These high-risk loans were then bundled into mortgage-backed securities (MBS) and sold to investors, spreading the risk throughout the 
financial system.

Compounding the problem was the role of credit rating agencies, which assigned high ratings to these complex financial products, often underestimating the risk involved. 
Investors, relying on these ratings, poured money into MBS and collateralized debt obligations (CDOs), further inflating the housing bubble. As housing prices continued to rise,
more people were enticed to take on mortgages they could not afford, believing that property values would keep climbing.

The situation was exacerbated by financial innovation and deregulation. The repeal of the Glass-Steagall Act in 1999 allowed commercial banks to engage in investment banking 
activities, blurring the lines between traditional banking and high-risk financial ventures. This deregulation, coupled with the rise of shadow banking systems—non-bank 
financial intermediaries that operate outside regular banking regulations—created an environment ripe for excessive risk-taking.

Government and Central Bank Actions
-----------------------------------

In response to the unfolding financial crisis, governments and central banks around the world took unprecedented measures to stabilize the economy and restore confidence in the 
financial system. The U.S. government, under the Bush administration, initiated the Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP) in October 2008, which allocated $700 billion to 
purchase toxic assets from banks and inject capital into financial institutions. This move aimed to shore up bank balance sheets and prevent a complete collapse of the banking 
sector.

Simultaneously, the Federal Reserve, led by Chairman Ben Bernanke, slashed interest rates to near zero and implemented a series of unconventional monetary policies. One of the 
most notable was the introduction of quantitative easing (QE), where the Fed purchased large quantities of government securities and mortgage-backed securities to inject 
liquidity into the financial system. This strategy was designed to lower long-term interest rates, encourage borrowing and investment, and support asset prices.
